The Problem

The businesses that need AI most are the ones that can't use it.

ChatGPT-style tools are genuinely useful, and for a large group of businesses, effectively off-limits. Not because the AI isn't good enough, but because of three things public AI tools can't fix from the outside:

Confidentiality

"We can't send that data out."

Mining suppliers sign NDAs with Vale and Glencore. Clinics hold health records under Ontario's PHIPA. Law and accounting firms hold privileged client files. Municipalities hold resident data. For all of them, pasting internal documents into a public chatbot ranges from against policy to against the law, and it's often happening anyway, unmanaged, as "shadow AI."

Buried knowledge

"The answer exists. Somewhere."

Twenty years of safety procedures, equipment manuals, past tenders, HR policies, and project files sit in shared drives and inboxes. The person who knows where everything is eventually retires. New staff take months to stop asking. Public AI can't help here at all: it has never seen your documents.

Unverifiable answers

"Says who?"

When an AI answer feeds a real decision (a bid price, a safety call, a compliance report), "the AI said so" isn't an acceptable source. Most AI tools give you an answer with no way to check where it came from, and no log of who asked what. In audited and regulated environments, that's disqualifying on its own.

Each problem is annoying alone. Together they mean: the data-heavy, confidentiality-bound businesses that would benefit most from AI are the least served by the current AI market. That's the gap OpenNorth is built for.

What OpenNorth Is

A private AI assistant, installed where your data already lives.

OpenNorth is a solo-founder studio in Sudbury that builds and runs private AI systems for one company at a time. The system runs on infrastructure you control, reads only the documents and data you connect to it, and produces answers with citations. Three capabilities, in order of maturity:

1 · Answer

Ask your documents questions

Staff ask in plain English ("What's the lockout procedure for the 980 loader?", "What did we bid on the 2023 ventilation contract?") and get an answer assembled only from your connected documents, scoped by each person's access permissions. No internet guessing.

2 · Prove

Every answer shows its sources

Each answer cites the exact documents and passages it came from; click through and verify. Every query is logged: who asked, what was retrieved, which model version answered. That log is an audit trail, which is now a legal expectation in places like Ontario health care (PHIPA requires electronic audit logs).

3 · Automate

Recurring work becomes a draft

The reports, summaries, and digests your team rebuilds every week or month become scheduled jobs: the system pulls fresh data, drafts the output, and a human reviews instead of assembles. This layer comes after Answer and Prove are trusted, not before.

What the AI is not allowed to do: answer from its own memory and present it as fact about your business. If the answer isn't in your connected data, the honest output is "I couldn't find this in your documents," with the closest matches shown. That restraint is a feature, and it's the difference between a demo and a system people trust.

  • Magic on day one. The first weeks are plumbing: collecting, cleaning, and indexing your documents properly

FAQ

The questions everyone asks. Answered like an engineer.

Is this just ChatGPT with my files uploaded?

No, on three counts. Where it runs: in your environment; nothing goes to an AI vendor, which is the whole point for NDA- and privacy-bound data. How it answers: only from your connected documents, with the sources cited on every answer; if the answer isn't in your data, it says so. What it remembers: every query is logged (who asked, what was retrieved, which model answered), so there's an audit trail, not a black box.

Do you train an AI model from scratch for us?

No, and be suspicious of anyone who says they will. Training a frontier model costs tens of millions of dollars. "Your own AI" here means: a proven open-weight model (MIT/Apache-licensed), deployed privately for you, grounded in your documents via retrieval, optionally fine-tuned on your vocabulary where it measurably helps. You get ownership and privacy without the training bill; that's the honest version of this product, from anyone selling it.

Are open models actually good enough?

For this job, yes, verifiably, as of mid-2026. Open-weight models (GLM-5.2, DeepSeek V4, the Qwen3 family) now lead or match closed models on independent leaderboards, and document Q&A with retrieval is one of the least demanding tasks you can ask of them: the heavy lifting is in the retrieval and data plumbing, not raw model IQ. Where frontier closed models still lead (the very hardest reasoning and agentic work), that's not what this system does. And the model is a swappable part: when a better open model ships next quarter, your deployment upgrades without changing anything else.

What about hallucinations?

They can't be eliminated; anyone claiming otherwise is selling something. They can be contained: the model only answers from retrieved passages of your documents, every answer carries its citations so a human can verify in one click, and "I couldn't find this in your documents" is a first-class answer. The POC's evaluation report measures exactly this, on your questions, with the misses shown to you, not just the wins.

Why not Microsoft Copilot? We already have Microsoft 365.

Honest answer: if your world is entirely in M365 and per-seat pricing works for you, Copilot is a reasonable tool. I'll say so in the first call if it's true. It falls short where OpenNorth focuses: it can't run on-premise or offline, it only reaches data living in Microsoft's ecosystem, per-seat costs compound at scale, and you get limited control over grounding, auditability, and which model runs. If those don't matter to you, you may not need me.

Can it really run with no internet at all?

Yes. Open-weight models are downloaded artifacts; they run wherever the hardware is. My own proof of concept (OreAcle) runs sensor ingest, ML anomaly detection, and a local-LLM copilot fully offline, built for underground mining conditions. Your office is an easier problem than a mine.

You're one person. What happens if you disappear?

The stack is open-source end to end: no proprietary components, no licenses that die with me. Everything is documented for handoff from day one, and any competent engineer can operate it. Compare that with the actual alternative risk: a cloud AI vendor changing prices, models, or terms under you with 30 days' notice. I'd argue the boring open stack is the lower-risk option, but the honest mitigation is that you own everything, always.
